One-sided limits 17 May 2022

Key ideas

The limit rule

The \(\lim\limits_{x \to a}f(x)\) exists if \(\lim\limits_{x \to a^-}f(x)\) is equal to \(\lim\limits_{x \to a^+}f(x)\)

Else, \(\lim\limits_{x \to a}f(x)\) does not exist.

  • A limit from the right: \(\lim\limits_{x \to a^+}f(x)\) (positive)
  • A limit from the left: \(\lim\limits_{x \to a^-}f(x)\) (negative)
  • \(f(a)\) is the y-value where \(x=a\)

Steps for evaluating a one-sided limit using a graph:

  1. Go to the coordinate \((x \to a)\) in the direction of the limit. For example, if the limit is \((x \to a^-\), go to the left.
  2. See what y-coordinate it approaches.
  3. If both the limits from the left and right are equal, the "normal" limit is the answer.
  4. If you see \(g(a)\): what y-coordinate is at \(x=a\)?

When to use one-sided limit rule

\(\displaystyle{\lim\limits_{x \to a^{\pm}}f(x)=\frac{\text{non-}0}{0}}\)

Steps for evaluating a one-sided limit using calculus:

  1. Substitute into the limit.
  2. If you get a non-zero over a zero: find a number close to \(x \to a\) (go up or down 0.1 depending on direction of limit) and substitute that number into the denominator.
  3. Divide the numerator by the denominator, and see if the result is positive or negative.
    1. If positive: the answer is \(+\infty\).
    2. If negative: the answer is \(-\infty\)

Sample problems

  1. \(\displaystyle{\lim\limits_{x \to 4^-}\frac{x^2+2}{4-x}}\)
    2. \[\begin{align} &=\frac{(4)^2+2}{4-(4)} \\ &=\frac{18}{0} \end{align}\]

    Now, we will have to find a number very close to 4 to the left. This is because the sign on the limit is negative. We'll solve this problem by inputting that number into the denominator only. We can choose 3.9 as our number because it is a number close to 4 that is to the left of it.

    \[\begin{align} &=4-(3.9) \\ &=+1.9 \\ &=\frac{18}{+} \\ &=+\infty \end{align}\]
  2. \(\displaystyle{\lim\limits_{x \to -1^+}\frac{x+8}{x+1}}\)
    3. \[\begin{align} &=\frac{(-1)+8}{(-1)+1} \\ &=\frac{7}{0} \end{align}\]

    We have the same problem again—the result was a non-zero over zero. So, we'll have to follow our steps again. We can choose -0.9 as our number because it is a number close to -1 that is to the right of it.

    \[\begin{align} &=(-0.9)+1 \\ &=0.1 \\ &=\frac{7}{+} \\ &=+\infty \end{align}\]
  3. \(\displaystyle{\lim\limits_{x \to 4^+}\frac{3x}{x^2-16}}\)
    4. \[\begin{align} &=\frac{3(4)}{(4)^2-16} \\ &=\frac{12}{0} \\ &=((4.1)^2-16) \\ &=0.81 \\ &=\frac{12}{+} \\ &=+\infty \end{align}\]

    Now, let's take a look at the same limit (basically) but from the left this time.

    \(\displaystyle{\lim\limits_{x \to 4^-}\frac{3x}{x^2-16}}\)

    \[\begin{align} &=\frac{3(4)}{(4)^2-16} \\ &=\frac{12}{0} \\ &=((3.9)^2-16) \\ &=-0.79 \\ &=\frac{12}{-} \\ &=-\infty \end{align}\]
