Chapter 6-1: Critical Values

This page is a note sheet for Chapter 6-1 of The PreCalculus Text. Learn how to find critical values, use critical values to find extreme points, and review derivatives. The material on this page is adapted from in-class notes and the textbook, available here.

Vocabulary

critical value: x-coordinate of an extreme point on a graph slopes are 0 at minimum or maximum points on a smooth curve

if slope\(=0\), the derivative\(=0\)

Find Critical Values

  1. Set \(\frac{dy}{dx}=0\), then solve for \(x\).
  2. Find where \(\frac{dy}{dx}=0\) does not exist. (Not applicable in Chapter 6.)
  3. Consider the endpoints of a given domain (if applicable).

Find Extreme Points

  1. Find the critical values.
  2. Plug the critical values into the original function individually. So, if your values are 3, 6, and 10, you need to input those values into the original equation 3 times.

Example 1

Find the critical value(s) of \(y=x^2-9x+20\).

1. Set the derivative to 0 and solve for \(x\).

\[\begin{aligned} \frac{dy}{dx}=2x-9\\ 2x-9=0 \\ x=4.5 \\ \end{aligned}\] Take the derivative of the original function.

2. Find where the derivative does not exist.

This aspect of the three-step process is not applicable in Chapter 6.

3. Consider the endpoints of the given domain.

This aspect of the three-step process is not applicable because no domain is given.

Final answer

The critical value for the given function is \(x=4.5\).

Example 2

Find the extreme point(s) of \(y=x^3-6x^2+22\).

1. Set the derivative to 0 and solve for \(x\).

\[\begin{aligned} \frac{dy}{dx}=3x^2-12x\\ 3x^2-12x=0 \\ x=0, x=4 \\ \end{aligned}\] Take the derivative of the original function.

2. Find where the derivative does not exist.

This aspect of the three-step process is not applicable in Chapter 6.

3. Consider the endpoints of the given domain.

This aspect of the three-step process is not applicable because no domain is given.

Final answer

\[\begin{aligned} y=0^3-6(0)^2+22\rightarrow(0,22) \\ y=4^3-6(4)^2+22\rightarrow(4,-10) \\ \end{aligned}\] Because we had to look for the extreme points, we had to plug in the x values.

The extreme points for the given function are \((0,22), (4,-10)\).

Example 3

Find the extreme point(s) of \(y=2x^3-3x^2-12x+10\) within the domain of \(x\epsilon[-4,4]\).

1. Set the derivative to 0 and solve for \(x\).

\[\begin{aligned} \frac{dy}{dx}=6x^2-6x-12\\ 6x^2-6x-12=0 \\ 6(x^2-x-2) \\ x=-1, x=2 \\ \end{aligned}\] Take the derivative of the original function. Factor by GCF first.

2. Find where the derivative does not exist.

This aspect of the three-step process is not applicable in Chapter 6.

3. Consider the endpoints of the given domain.

The answer to this is simple: just write the values in the domain.

Endpoints: \(x=-4, x=4\)

Do this step first. When you go to complete Step 1 after this step, you can see if the values you found are within the range. If they aren't, then don't include them.

Final answer

\[\begin{aligned} y=2(-4)^3-3(-4)^2-12(-4)+10\rightarrow(-4,-118) \\ y=2(-1)^3-3(-1)^2-12(-1)+10\rightarrow(-1,21) \\ y=2(2)^3-3(2)^2-12(2)+10\rightarrow(2,-10) \\ y=2(4)^3-3(4)^2-12(4)+10\rightarrow(4,42) \\ \end{aligned}\] Because we had to look for the extreme points, we had to plug in the x values.

The extreme points for the given function are \((-4,-118), (-1,21), (2,-10), (4,42)\).

Example 4

Find the extreme point(s) of \(y=\frac{1}{3}x^3+\frac{1}{2}x^2-12x+22\).

1. Set the derivative to 0 and solve for \(x\).

\[\begin{aligned} \frac{dy}{dx}=x^2+x-12 \\ x^2+x-12=0 \\ x=-4, x=3 \\ \end{aligned}\] Take the derivative of the original function.

2. Find where the derivative does not exist.

This aspect of the three-step process is not applicable in Chapter 6.

3. Consider the endpoints of the given domain.

This aspect of the three-step process is not applicable because no domain is given.

Final answer

\[\begin{aligned} y=\frac{1}{3}(-4)^3+\frac{1}{2}(-4)^2-12(-4)+22\rightarrow(-4,56\frac{1}{3}) \\ y=\frac{1}{3}(3)^3+\frac{1}{2}(3)^2-12(3)+22\rightarrow(3,\frac{1}{2}) \\ \end{aligned}\] Because we had to look for the extreme points, we had to plug in the x values.

The extreme points for the given function are \((-4,56\frac{1}{3}),(3,\frac{1}{2})\).

Example 5

Find the extreme point(s) of \(f(x)=x^4-8x^2+12\) within the domain of \(x\epsilon[0,10]\).

1. Set the derivative to 0 and solve for \(x\).

\[\begin{aligned} \frac{dy}{dx}=4x^3-16x\\ 4x^3-16x=0 \\ 4x(x^2-4)=0 \\ 4x(x-2)(x+2)=0 \\ x=-2, x=0, x=2 \\ \end{aligned}\] Take the derivative of the original function. Factor by GCF first.

Now, let's look at the domain. It says that our values have to be between \(0\) and \(10\), but one of our answers is \(-2\). Therefore, disregard \(-2\); we won't be using it.

2. Find where the derivative does not exist.

This aspect of the three-step process is not applicable in Chapter 6.

3. Consider the endpoints of the given domain.

The answer to this is simple: just write the values in the domain.

Endpoints: \(x=0, x=10\)

Final answer

\[\begin{aligned} f(x)=0^4-8(0)^2+12\rightarrow(0,12) \\ f(x)=2^4-8(2)^2+12\rightarrow(2,-4) \\ f(x)=10^4-8(10)^2+12\rightarrow(10,9212) \\ \end{aligned}\] Because we had to look for the extreme points, we had to plug in the x values.

The extreme points for the given function are \((0,12), (2,-4), (10,9212)\).
