Chemical or Physical?

There are two types of properties and changes: Chemical and Physical. But first, let's look at matter and mass.

  • Matter is anything that takes up space and has mass and volume. An example is a desk, water, and the air. Yes, even the air!
  • Mass is how much matter is in a given area. Mass does not mean weight.

Now let's look at chemical and physical properties.

  • A physical property is something that can be observed without changing an object's identity.
    • Often times, this includes observations based on your five senses.
    • For example: texture, color, boiling point, shape, size, melting point, current phase, freezing point.

  • Phases of Matter (physical property)

    • Solid: definite volume and shape, particles packed in a fixed position.
    • Liquid: definite volume but indefinite shape, particles are close together, but not in a fixed position.
    • Gas: indefinite volume and shape, particles are at a greater distance from each other.
  • A chemical property is generally an object's ability to transform into a new substance.
    • It describes how a substance reacts, or doesn't react, to another substance.
    • For example: Gas formation (not by boiling), changing to a solid (not by freezing, precipitate), gain or loss of energy, new color
