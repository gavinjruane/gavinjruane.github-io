MATH 142 1.4: Work

1.4: Work

Work By the physical definition, it is defined as \(W=\text{force}\times\text{distance}\)

If we consider the distance over a short spatial step called \(\Delta x\), we can rewrite the definition, replacing "distance" with \(\Delta x\).

Generally, force is not constant and dependent on the position, so it can be represented as \(F=F(x)\)

So, the overall work is given by the sum \[\sum_i F_i(x)\Delta x\]

If we set \(\Delta x\) to be infinitely small, it will be the limit of the above sum as \(\Delta x\rightarrow 0\). This is better shown as the integral: \[W=\int_a^b F(x)dx\]

Force equation The force equation can take many different forms.

  • Object weight \(F=mg\) where \(m\) represents mass and \(g\) represents gravity.
  • Hooke's law For a spring of natural length \(x_0\), the force required to extend it \(x\) distance is represented by: \[F(x)=kx\]where \(k\) represents the spring constant.

Example using a spring

A natural length for a spring is 0.2m. A force of 50 Newtons is required to extend the spring to 0.5m. How much work is done extending the spring from 0.5m to 1.0m?

\[ \begin{align} a&=0.5-0.2=0.3 \\ b&=1.0-0.2=0.8 \end{align} \] \[ \begin{align} W&=\int_{0.3}^{0.8} (kx)dx \\ &=\left[\frac{kx^2}{2}\right]_{0.3}^{0.8} \\ &=\left[\frac{k(0.8)^2}{2}\right]-\left[\frac{k(0.3)^2}{2}\right] \\ &=0.32k-0.045k \\ &=0.275k \end{align} \] \[ \begin{align} 50&=0.3k \\ k&=166.667 \end{align} \] \[ \begin{align} W&=0.275(166.667) \\ W&=45.833 \end{align} \]
