I've been thinking about the whole idea of tanning lately. Yes, I'm aware that's very random.

In all seriousness: the idea of tanning is such a strange one. Tanning is particularly common among white people, who are privileged by virtue of their skin color. Historically (and still today in more covert ways), Anglos in the United States have shunned people for their darker skin tones and characterized colors other than white as inferior.

Yet, white people—including me—love to tan in the summer months and attain that dark, golden tone glamorized by all forms of media. Some white people will go as far as to say to their nonwhite friends, "Oh, I'm almost as dark as you!" when firstly, they most definitely are not; and secondly, they are being undeniably racist.

For many white people, tanning is—whether consciously or not—an opportunity to capitalize on the "glamour" of darker skin while never having to face the reality of being a person of color. Whites get to try on a new, exotic look for the summer months without sacrificing their privilege and then shed it when it's no longer in season—literally.

Is tanning inherently wrong? Oh my god, of course not; and at the end of the day, it's inevitable if one spends time out in the sun. However, it's important for us white people to think about our place in context with nonwhite people in our society.
